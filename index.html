Moon Garden Seed Bombs & Seed Paper
Understanding these fantastic ideas: https://ziti.works/L15DIY_Playlist

Seed Selection:

  1. Purple Coneflower (Echinacea purpurea) - This native perennial plant produces purple to pink daisy-like flowers with a prominent cone-shaped center. The flowers can have a striking appearance in moonlight, and they are known to attract pollinators, such as butterflies and bees, during the day.
  2. Wild Bergamot (Monarda fistulosa) - Also known as bee balm or wild mint, this native perennial plant produces clusters of lavender to purple flowers with a unique tubular shape. The flowers are often fragrant and can be attractive to pollinators, including bees and hummingbirds, during the day and potentially during the evening as well.
  3. Wild Lupine (Lupinus perennis) - This native perennial plant produces tall spikes of blue to purple pea-like flowers. The flowers can be visually striking and may be visible in moonlight. Wild Lupine is also a host plant for the larvae of the Eastern Tailed-Blue butterfly, which can add to the ecological value of a moon garden.
  4. Lanceleaf Coreopsis (Coreopsis lanceolata) - This native perennial plant produces bright yellow daisy-like flowers with lance-shaped leaves. The yellow flowers can provide a contrasting color against the dark evening or night-time backdrop, and they are known to attract pollinators, such as bees and butterflies, during the day.
  5. Wild Columbine (Aquilegia canadensis) - This native perennial plant produces unique red and yellow flowers that hang down from slender stems. The flowers have an interesting shape and coloration, and they can be visually appealing in a moon garden. Wild Columbine is also known to attract hummingbirds and bees during the day.
  6. Virginia Bluebell (Mertensia virginica) - This native perennial plant produces clusters of bell-shaped flowers that start out pink and mature to blue-purple. The flowers can have a delicate and ethereal appearance, and they may be visually appealing in moonlight. Virginia Bluebell is also known to attract pollinators, such as bees and butterflies, during the day.

These 5 seed verity are likely to be native to most all of the US, and will bloom rather easily producing beautiful lunarpunk-esk flowers that are fragrent and/or interesting at night time and especially under a full moon.

Seed Bombs:

Materials needed:
  • Seeds of your choice (e.g., Purple Coneflower, Wild Bergamot, Wild Lupine, Lanceleaf Coreopsis, Wild Columbine, Virginia Bluebell)
  • Clay or clay-rich soil
  • Compost or potting soil
  • Water
  • Mixing bowl
  • Wax paper or parchment paper
  • Optional: natural dye or food coloring (if desired)
Instructions:
  1. In a mixing bowl, combine the seeds with clay or clay-rich soil in a ratio of approximately 1:3 or 1:4 (seeds to soil). The clay or soil helps to bind the seeds together and protect them from drying out.
  2. Add a small amount of compost or potting soil to the mixture to provide additional nutrients for germination.
  3. Slowly add water while stirring the mixture until it reaches a dough-like consistency that can be easily formed into balls.
  4. Take small handfuls of the mixture and roll them into balls about 1 inch in diameter. Place the seed bombs on wax paper or parchment paper and allow them to dry for at least 24 hours.
  5. Once dry, the seed bombs can be distributed in suitable outdoor areas, such as gardens, meadows, or along hiking trails. The rain or watering will gradually dissolve the clay, allowing the seeds to germinate and grow into new plants.

Seed Paper:

Materials needed:
  • Seeds of your choice (e.g., Purple Coneflower, Wild Bergamot, Wild Lupine, Lanceleaf Coreopsis, Wild Columbine, Virginia Bluebell)
  • Recycled paper (such as newspaper or scrap paper)
  • Blender or food processor
  • Water
  • Large bowl or basin
  • Fine mesh screen (such as a window screen)
  • Sponge or cloth
  • Rolling pin or flat object
  • Optional: natural dye or food coloring (if desired)

Instructions:

  1. Tear the recycled paper into small pieces and place them in a blender or food processor.
  2. Add enough water to cover the paper pieces and let them soak for several hours or overnight until they become soft.
  3. Blend or process the paper and water mixture until it becomes a pulp-like consistency.
  4. Transfer the paper pulp to a large bowl or basin filled with water. Stir the mixture gently with your hands to evenly distribute the pulp in the water.
  5. Place the fine mesh screen over another basin or sink. Slowly pour the paper pulp mixture onto the screen, allowing the water to drain through the mesh.
  6. Use a sponge or cloth to press and remove as much water as possible from the paper pulp on the screen.
  7. Sprinkle the seeds of your choice evenly onto the paper pulp, pressing them gently into the pulp with your fingers or the sponge.
  8. Place another piece of cloth or parchment paper on top of the seeded paper pulp, and use a rolling pin or a flat object to press and flatten the mixture, evenly distributing the seeds within the paper.
  9. Carefully peel off the top cloth or parchment paper, leaving the seeded paper on the mesh screen to dry for at least 24 hours.
  10. Once dry, the seed paper can be cut into desired shapes, such as squares, circles, or custom shapes, and used for planting in suitable outdoor areas. The paper will biodegrade over time, and the seeds will germinate and grow into new plants.

Note: It's important to research and choose appropriate planting locations that are suitable for the selected native plant species. Proper care and maintenance of the planted seeds and paper is also important to ensure successful germination and growth of the native plants. Watering, sunlight, and protection from harsh weather conditions may be necessary depending on the specific requirements of the selected plant species.


Additionally, when using natural dyes or food coloring to color the seed bombs or seed paper, it's important to choose non-toxic, eco-friendly options that won't harm the environment or the seeds. Avoid using synthetic or chemical dyes that may be harmful to the plants, wildlife, or soil.
Overall, making seed bombs and seed paper can be a fun and eco-friendly way to propagate native plants and contribute to the conservation of local ecosystems.

By using the selected seeds of Purple Coneflower, Wild Bergamot, Wild Lupine, Lanceleaf Coreopsis, Wild Columbine, and Virginia Bluebell, you can create seed bombs and seed paper that can potentially grow into beautiful native flowers, providing habitat and food for local wildlife and contributing to the overall biodiversity of the area.
